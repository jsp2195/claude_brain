# About Me

## Who I Am

Jarett Poliner – Postdoctoral Researcher at Los Alamos National Laboratory.

I work at the intersection of AI, computational mechanics, and materials science, developing generative and predictive models that connect material microstructure to mechanical behavior.

## Background

- B.S. in Engineering Mechanics, Columbia University
- M.S. and Ph.D. in Civil Engineering, Columbia University
- Doctoral focus: generative modeling for solid mechanics in sparse-data, physics-constrained settings
- Current research: learning physically meaningful representations of complex heterogeneous materials — analyzing, generating, and predicting material response

## Positioning

My distinctive angle is building generative and representation-learning methods at the boundary of mechanics, materials, and modern machine learning. The core contribution is not just using ML as a predictor, but using it to learn physically meaningful structure in systems where data are sparse, expensive, and constrained by underlying mechanics. Generative modeling for solid mechanics and materials in sparse-data settings is the specific niche — where purely data-hungry approaches tend to break down.

## Professional Values

- **Rigor**: Models must be grounded in the actual structure of the problem, not just produce impressive-looking results.
- **Physical interpretability**: Methods should respect and encode the physics of the system, not abstract it away.
- **Methodological honesty**: No overstating what a model can do. Uncertainty and mechanism matter.
- **Reproducibility**: Work should be transparent and repeatable.
- **Interdisciplinary integration**: The best work genuinely combines mechanics, simulation, materials science, and machine learning rather than treating them as separate silos.

## Not This

- Not black-box ML that ignores physical structure.
- Not AI hype for its own sake.
- Not models detached from mechanism, uncertainty, or scientific utility.
- The goal is never just "apply AI to science" — it is to build methods that are scientifically meaningful, technically rigorous, and genuinely informative for hard physical problems.
