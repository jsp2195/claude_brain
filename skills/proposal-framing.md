# Skill: Proposal and Project Framing

Covers turning messy research ideas into clearly scoped, fundable project narratives, milestones, and technical plans.

---

## What This Is For

The hardest part of proposal writing is not the prose — it's the framing. This skill covers the structural and strategic work of shaping a research direction into a coherent, defensible, and compelling project:

- Defining the technical problem clearly
- Scoping what's in and what's out
- Sequencing milestones so they build logically
- Positioning the work against a funding context (e.g., GENESIS mission priorities)
- Making the "why now" and "why this team" arguments legible

## Structure Template

Most of Jarett's proposals follow some version of this logic:

1. **Problem**: What physical/scientific problem is unsolved and why does it matter?
2. **Gap**: What's missing in current approaches? (Be specific — not "nobody has done X" but "existing methods fail when Y because Z.")
3. **Approach**: What is the proposed technical method? How does it address the gap?
4. **Architecture**: What are the key components and how do they connect? (Latent models, generative models, physics constraints, multi-fidelity integration, UQ, etc.)
5. **Milestones**: What will be delivered, in what order, and how will progress be measured?
6. **Team and resources**: Who does what and why is this group the right one?
7. **Broader impact / mission alignment**: How does this connect to the funder's priorities?

## Voice Rules

- Confident but not overclaiming. "This approach enables X" is fine. "This will revolutionize Y" is not.
- Technically precise. Reviewers are domain experts — do not hand-wave.
- Frame novelty specifically: what is new, what is adapted, what is established.
- Use Jarett's natural framing: physically meaningful representations, sparse-data settings, mechanistically grounded methods, structure-property relationships.
- Avoid: "paradigm shift," "transformative," "revolutionary," "game-changing," "novel" without justification, or any framing that sounds like marketing.

## Process

1. Start by clarifying the core research question — what is the one sentence this proposal is really about?
2. Map the technical architecture before writing prose. What are the components and how do they interact?
3. Identify the strongest "why this matters" argument for the specific funding context.
4. Draft milestones that are concrete and measurable, not vague aspirations.
5. Stress-test: what would a skeptical reviewer push back on? Address it preemptively.

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] The core research question is stated in one clear sentence
- [ ] The gap is specific and defensible, not a strawman
- [ ] The approach directly addresses the stated gap
- [ ] Milestones are concrete, sequenced, and measurable
- [ ] Novelty claims are specific — what's new vs. what's adapted vs. what's standard
- [ ] The framing fits the funding context (mission alignment, priorities, language)
- [ ] No overclaiming — uncertainty and limitations are acknowledged
- [ ] The technical architecture is clear enough that a reviewer can evaluate feasibility
- [ ] Team justification is substantive, not generic
