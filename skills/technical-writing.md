# Skill: Technical Writing

Covers papers, abstracts, methods sections, proposal text, and research summaries.

---

## Format

- **Papers/methods sections**: LaTeX-ready prose. Structured by logical flow, not by chronology of how the work was done. Each paragraph should have a clear job.
- **Abstracts**: 150–250 words unless otherwise specified. State the problem, the method, what's new, and what the result means — in that order.
- **Research summaries**: Markdown. Compress without losing substance. Lead with the takeaway, not the setup.
- **Proposal text**: Clean prose, no bullet points unless the call format requires them. See `proposal-framing.md` for narrative structure.

## Voice Rules

- Direct, precise, technically dense but logically legible.
- No filler, no hedging for politeness, no motivational framing.
- Every sentence earns its place. If a sentence can be cut without losing information, cut it.
- Use Jarett's natural vocabulary: "physically meaningful," "mechanistically grounded," "sparse-data setting," "structure-property relationships."
- Do not use: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "disruptive," "novel" (unless substantively justified), or any phrasing that sounds like generic AI evangelism.
- Frame contributions in terms of what the method actually does, what assumptions it makes, and why the result is scientifically useful.

## Process

1. If editing existing text: make surgical changes. Do not rewrite what doesn't need rewriting.
2. If drafting new text: produce a strong, complete first pass — not a tentative sketch. Make it modular and easy to refine.
3. Keep the logic legible to adjacent fields (e.g., a materials scientist reading an ML-heavy methods section, or an ML researcher reading mechanics-constrained formulation).
4. When claiming novelty, be specific about what is new and what is not. Never overstate.

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Every claim is grounded — no vague assertions of novelty or importance
- [ ] No fabricated citations, results, or implementation details
- [ ] Technical terms are used precisely (check glossary.md if uncertain)
- [ ] The text is legible to someone from an adjacent field
- [ ] No unnecessary padding or redundancy
- [ ] Uncertainty is stated where it exists
- [ ] Notation and terminology are internally consistent
- [ ] The logical structure is clear — each section/paragraph has a defined role
