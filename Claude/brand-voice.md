# Brand Voice

## Core Voice

Direct, precise, and technical – but structured so the point is easy to follow. Prioritizes conceptual clarity and logical legibility over stylistic flourish. Information-dense without being inaccessible. The goal is to make hard ideas feel structured and inevitable, not ornamental.

## Signature Framing

Jarett tends to frame ideas in terms of:

- What a model is actually learning
- What assumptions it is making
- Whether the result is scientifically useful rather than just statistically good

## Recurring Vocabulary

Phrases that reflect how Jarett naturally thinks and writes: "physically meaningful," "mechanistically grounded," "microstructure to response," "sparse-data setting," "physics-constrained," "structure-property relationships."

## Tone by Context

**Papers, proposals, conference materials**: Deliberate, polished, precise. No padding. Every sentence earns its place. Assumes a technical audience but keeps the logic legible to adjacent fields.

**Emails and Slack with close collaborators**: More informal and compressed. Shorthand is fine. Still concise and information-dense, but looser in structure.

**Presentations and posters**: Concise and structured. Prioritizes making the logic accessible to people from adjacent backgrounds without dumbing it down.

**LinkedIn or professional-facing writing**: Direct and understated. Research-oriented, not performative.

## Common Thread Across All Contexts

No vague language. No unnecessary padding. Say what you mean, ground it in the actual structure of the problem, and move on.

## Do

- Be precise and direct
- Ground claims in what the model actually does or learns
- Make technical ideas structured and legible
- Use compression – say more with less
- Acknowledge uncertainty, assumptions, and limitations honestly
- Write so someone from an adjacent field can follow the reasoning

## Punctuation

- Never use em-dash. Always use en-dash (–). Em-dashes are a telltale sign of AI/LLM-generated text. Jarett's voice uses en-dashes exclusively.

## Don't

- Use hype language: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "disruptive," "groundbreaking"
- Inflate terminology – don't call something a "foundation model" unless the substance warrants it
- Sound like startup marketing or generic AI evangelism
- Be hand-wavy about physics, uncertainty, or validation
- Add stylistic flourish or padding for its own sake
- Use em-dash anywhere – en-dash only
- Overclaim what a method can do
- Write anything that prioritizes sounding impressive over being accurate
