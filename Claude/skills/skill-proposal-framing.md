# Skill: Proposal and Project Framing

Covers turning messy research ideas into clearly scoped, fundable project narratives, milestones, and technical plans.

---

## What This Skill Is For

The hardest part of proposal writing is not the technical detail — it is framing. This skill covers converting a real but loosely organized research direction into a coherent project with a clear problem statement, a defensible approach, scoped milestones, and a narrative that communicates significance to reviewers who may not share Jarett's exact technical background.

## Structure Template

1. **Problem statement** — What is the scientific or engineering gap? Why does it matter? Be specific, not generic.
2. **Current limitations** — What do existing approaches fail to do, and why? This is where the proposal earns its right to exist.
3. **Proposed approach** — What will this project do, technically? What is the method, and why is it the right one for this problem?
4. **Key innovations** — What is genuinely new here? State this precisely and honestly. Do not overclaim.
5. **Technical plan / milestones** — What gets done, in what order, and what does success look like at each stage?
6. **Significance and impact** — Why does solving this problem matter beyond the immediate project? Connect to the funder's mission where relevant.
7. **Risk and mitigation** — What could go wrong, and what is the fallback?

Not every document needs all seven sections, but every proposal should address the underlying questions.

## Voice Rules

- Precise and confident, but never overclaimed. Reviewers can tell the difference.
- Frame the work in terms of what it actually enables, not what it aspires to in the abstract.
- Use concrete technical language rather than vague gestures at impact.
- When connecting to mission priorities (e.g., GENESIS, NNSA), be specific about the alignment rather than generic.
- Acknowledge what is hard and what remains open — this builds credibility rather than undermining it.

## Framing Patterns That Work

- **Gap - Method - Payoff**: "Current approaches to X fail because Y. We propose Z, which addresses this by [mechanism]. This enables [concrete outcome]."
- **From data to decision**: Position the work as closing a loop — from microstructure data to representation to prediction to design or decision.
- **Sparse-data as a feature, not a bug**: Frame the sparse-data setting as a design constraint that motivates the method, not as an excuse.

## Don't

- Use hype or inflated impact language ("transformative," "paradigm-shifting," "revolutionary").
- Be vague about what the project will actually produce.
- Claim novelty without being specific about what is new relative to existing work.
- Write milestones that are unmeasurable ("explore the design space," "investigate potential applications").
- Conflate the umbrella research direction with the specific funded scope.
- Ignore the reviewer's perspective — they need to understand why this matters and why it will work within one or two reads.

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] The problem statement is specific and grounded, not generic.
- [ ] The gap between current methods and what is needed is clearly articulated.
- [ ] The proposed approach is technically concrete — a reviewer can picture what will be built.
- [ ] Milestones are measurable and scoped to the project timeline.
- [ ] Novelty claims are precise and honest.
- [ ] The narrative connects to the funder's mission with specifics, not platitudes.
- [ ] Risks are acknowledged and addressed.
- [ ] The document reads clearly to a technically literate reviewer from an adjacent field.
- [ ] No fabricated references, inflated comparisons, or unsupported claims.
